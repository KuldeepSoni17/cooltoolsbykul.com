મારી હસ્તિ મારી પાછળ એ રીતે વિસરાઈ ગઈ

આંગળી જળમાંથી નીકળી, ને જગ્યા પુરાઈ ગઈ

Maari hasti maari paachhal e reete visaraai gayi —
aangli jal-maanthi nikli, ne jagya puraai gayi.
My existence, behind me, was forgotten like this:
a finger pulled out of water — and the space filled in.
— Mareez · 1917 – 1983
if you stayed, scroll

Mareez wrote that in a one-room flat in Bombay's Bhendi Bazaar. He was poor. He was an alcoholic. He never told most people he was writing. He died in 1983. You never heard of him.

Today's reading

A working definition of a good person

Narsinh Mehta નરસિંહ મહેતા c. 1414 – 1481 Read time · 4 minutes

વૈષ્ણવ જન તો તેને કહીએ,

જે પીડ પરાઈ જાણે રે

પરદુઃખે ઉપકાર કરે તોયે,

મન અભિમાન ન આણે રે

Vaishnav jan to tene kahiye,

je peed parai jaane re —

par-dukhe upakaar kare toye,

man abhimaan na aane re.

Call him the true one — that one —

who feels another's pain as his own.

Who eases the suffering of strangers,

and lets no pride enter his heart.

Where this comes from

Junagadh, mid-15th century. Narsinh Mehta — saint, widower, cast out of his Nagar Brahmin community for the unforgivable sin of singing alongside the untouchables. He has no patron, no temple, no caste, no defenders.

And so he writes a definition. It opens with the word Vaishnav, but listen to where it goes: not to a god, not to a ritual, not even to a temple. It goes to peed parai — another's pain. That's where the poem locates the holy.

Why it still matters

Gandhi made this his daily prayer. Not because he was theologically Vaishnav — he wasn't, particularly — but because of what comes after the word. The poem says goodness isn't measured by what you believe. It's measured by whose pain you can feel.

Six centuries later, the test still works. Try it on anyone you know. Try it on yourself. The hardest line, always, is the last — doing the kindness, then quietly refusing the credit.

The voices

Six poets, in one sentence each

Just enough to know whose room you're walking into. The reading comes after.

Bhakti
Narsinh Mehta
નરસિંહ મહેતા
c.1414 – 1481

The first voice in the language. An outcast saint who wrote definitions, not doctrines. Read him second — after someone modern — so you can feel how he got there first.

Ghazal
Mareez
મરીઝ
1917 – 1983

The Ghalib of Gujarati. Lived in poverty in a Bombay bylane, wrote couplets so precise that people now quote them as proverbs — without realising someone, somewhere, paid for each one in cigarettes and silence.

Romantic
Kalapi
કલાપી
1874 – 1900

A prince who died at twenty-six, leaving Gujarati its first modern love poems — the kind where the beloved isn't a goddess, just someone, and the speaker isn't asking for her, only noticing what she has done to him.

Humanist
Umashankar Joshi
ઉમાશંકર જોશી
1911 – 1988

Jnanpith laureate. Wrote in the wake of partition like a man trying to be larger than his own borders — and managed it more often than most of us manage on a good day.

Folk · Patriot
Jhaverchand Meghani
ઝવેરચંદ મેઘાણી
1896 – 1947

Travelled Saurashtra on foot, notebook in hand, collecting what the villages were already singing. Gandhi called him Rashtriya Shayar — the national bard — for poems that sound like a horse at full gallop.

Modern
Ramesh Parekh
રમેશ પારેખ
1940 – 2006

Wrote like a man who had just rediscovered his own body — and a woman named Sonal who may or may not have been real, and a Saurashtra that only exists when you're reading him.

Short Stories · ટૂંકી વાર્તાઓ

Three openings — no endings spoiled

Gujarati prose was quieter than Bengali, sparser than Malayalam, and at its best it could break a reader's wrist with a single sentence.

i.

The Post Office

પોસ્ટ ઑફિસ
Dhumketu · ધૂમકેતુ · 1923

An old man called Coachman Ali walks to the post office every dawn, sits on the same step, and asks the same question. For five years the postmaster has been annoyed by him. For five years the answer has been no. The story is about what the postmaster realises — and when.

ii.

A Tale of Humanity

માનવીની ભવાઈ
Pannalal Patel · પન્નાલાલ પટેલ · 1947

Two villagers, Kalu and Raju, want to marry each other. Then comes the Chhappaniya — the famine of 1900 — and the novel becomes something else. Not a love story interrupted by hunger. A love story made entirely of hunger, in which every meal is a moral decision.

iii.

Night of the Full Moon

પૂનમની રાત
Saroj Pathak · સરોજ પાઠક · 1960s

Saroj Pathak wrote about middle-class Gujarati women the way a surgeon writes about an organ they have decided to save. This story takes place over one evening, in one house, between one woman and her own reflection. Nothing happens. Everything is decided.
